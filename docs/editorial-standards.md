# Whisper Audio Tours — Editorial Standards

Version 1.0 | Last updated: 2026-02-16

This document defines the writing standards, quality checklist, and editorial rules for all curated audio tour content in the Whisper platform. All content — whether written by humans or generated by AI — must pass these standards before import into the story library.

---

## 1. Content Architecture

Each place in the tour system has **4 curated stories**, one per tone. Each story contains three parts:

| Field | Purpose | Constraints |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| `hook` | One-sentence surprising fact that stops the walker | Max 120 characters. Must be specific (names, dates, numbers). No vague superlatives. |
| `directional_cue` | Physical instruction tied to a visible feature | Must contain an action verb (look, stand, walk, find, watch, notice, face, turn). References real, currently visible features only. |
| `audio_narration` | The main spoken content, read aloud by TTS | 500–650 characters. One core idea. Conversational rhythm with varied sentence lengths. |

### Why these constraints

- **Hook at 120 chars**: Displayed as a card in the UI. Longer hooks wrap awkwardly on mobile.
- **Narration at 500–650 chars**: Produces 30–45 seconds of audio at natural speaking pace via ElevenLabs. Under 500 feels thin; over 650 loses attention.
- **One idea per narration**: Audio is linear — listeners can't re-read. A single focused idea lands; multiple ideas blur.

---

## 2. Tone System

Every story has exactly one tone. The four tones ensure that different user preferences are served for the same location.

### Tone definitions

| Tone | Voice | Sentence style | Example opener |
|------|-------|---------------|----------------|
| **scholarly** | Knowledgeable guide, not lecturing | Longer sentences with embedded detail. Precise language. | "The building dates to 1245, designed to rival the French cathedrals..." |
| **casual** | Friend who lives here | Short sentences, contractions, rhetorical questions. Direct address ("you"). | "So that flag up there tells you everything..." |
| **dramatic** | Storyteller building tension | Scene-setting, present-tense narration, vivid sensory detail. | "On Christmas Day 1066, William the Conqueror was crowned inside this abbey..." |
| **witty** | Sharp observer with a light touch | Irony, surprising juxtapositions, deadpan observations. Never cruel. | "Here's something most people miss..." |

### Tone rules

- Each place gets exactly 4 stories, one per tone.
- No two stories for the same place should repeat the same core fact.
- Tone must be consistent throughout the narration — don't start scholarly and end casual.
- Witty does not mean flippant. Every witty story must still contain at least one real, verifiable fact.

## 3. Interest Tags

Each story is tagged with 1–3 interests from the approved list. These are used by the matching algorithm to select the best story for each user.

### Approved interests

`history` · `architecture` · `food` · `art` · `culture` · `music` · `literature` · `science` · `local-life` · `photography` · `myths` · `politics` · `theatre` · `engineering` · `design` · `nature`

### Tagging rules

- Primary interest (first in array) must be the dominant theme of the narration.
- Avoid over-tagging. If the story mentions architecture in passing but is really about history, tag it `["history"]` not `["history", "architecture"]`.
- Aim for coverage: across 4 stories for one place, try to cover at least 6 distinct interests.
- Underrepresented interests (food, literature, music, science, nature) should be actively sought when the place supports them.

## 4. Writing Rules (Non-Negotiable)

These rules apply to all narrations regardless of tone.

### Structure
1. **One idea per narration.** A single focused story, anecdote, or observation. Not a summary of everything about the place.
2. **Open with the strongest line.** The first sentence must justify the listener's attention.
3. **End with resonance.** The last sentence should leave an image, a feeling, or a connection to what the listener is seeing. Never end with a generic wrap-up.

### Language
4. **No travel-writing clichés.** Banned phrases: "hidden gem", "best-kept secret", "step back in time", "rich tapestry", "bustling", "vibrant", "iconic", "steeped in history", "nestled", "picturesque", "a must-see", "breathtaking".
5. **Specific over general.** Always prefer "13.7 tonnes" over "very heavy". Prefer "1913" over "early 20th century". Prefer "Portland stone" over "stone".
6. **Contractions are fine.** Audio narration should sound spoken, not written. Use "it's", "they're", "wasn't" freely.
7. **No filler phrases.** Remove "it's worth noting that", "interestingly", "as a matter of fact", "it goes without saying".

### Accuracy
8. **Every fact must be verifiable.** Dates, names, measurements, and claims must be checkable. If uncertain, cut it.
9. **Attribute opinions.** "Prince Charles called it a nuclear power station" is fine. "It's ugly" is not.
10. **Current state matters.** Directional cues must reference features that exist today. Don't send people to look at something that was demolished.

### Audio-specific
11. **Vary sentence length.** A mix of short and long sentences creates natural spoken rhythm. Three long sentences in a row sounds like a textbook.
12. **Avoid parentheticals.** In written text, parentheses work. In audio, they derail the listener. Restructure as separate sentences.
13. **Numbers in context.** "240 bedrooms" means nothing alone. "Count the windows — there are 240 bedrooms behind them" connects the number to something visible.

## 5. Quality Checklist

Before any story is imported into the database, it must pass every item:

- [ ] Narration is 500–650 characters
- [ ] Hook is under 120 characters
- [ ] Hook contains at least one specific detail (name, date, or number)
- [ ] Directional cue references a currently visible, real-world feature
- [ ] Directional cue contains an action verb
- [ ] Tone is consistent throughout (no tone-shifting mid-narration)
- [ ] One core idea only — not a summary of the place
- [ ] No banned clichés
- [ ] All facts are verifiable
- [ ] Last sentence creates resonance, not a generic conclusion
- [ ] Interests array accurately reflects the narration content
- [ ] No repeated facts across the 4 stories for the same place

---

## 6. Validation Pipeline

The import system (`import-city-data` edge function) enforces:

- Required fields: `place_id`, `interests`, `tone`, `hook`, `directional_cue`, `audio_narration`
- Tone must be one of: `casual`, `scholarly`, `dramatic`, `witty`
- `interests` must be an array
- Narration length: 400–700 characters (wider than editorial target to allow edge cases)
- No duplicate `place_id` + `tone` + primary interest combinations

Editorial review (human or automated) enforces the stricter 500–650 char range and qualitative rules above.

---

## 7. Content Generation with AI

When using AI to draft stories:

1. **Prompt with the tone definitions above.** Include example openers.
2. **Provide the place context:** name, neighborhood, category, coordinates, and any known facts.
3. **Request all 4 tones at once** so the AI can avoid repeating facts across tones.
4. **Always review output** against the quality checklist. AI drafts frequently:
   - Exceed character limits (trim the weakest sentence)
   - Use clichés ("hidden gem", "iconic")
   - Pack in too many facts (cut to one idea)
   - Write hooks that are observations rather than surprising facts
5. **Validate directional cues** against current Street View or recent photos. AI may reference features that no longer exist.

## 8. Story Library Schema Reference

```
cities (id, slug, name, country)
  └── city_places (place_id PK, city_id FK, name, lat, lng, trigger_radius_m, category, must_see, neighborhood)
       └── place_stories (place_id FK, interests TEXT[], tone, hook, directional_cue, audio_narration)
```

- `place_stories` has a GIN index on `interests` for fast array matching
- The matching algorithm scores stories by tone match (+10) and interest overlap (+5 per match)
- If no curated story matches, the system falls back to GPT-4o generation
